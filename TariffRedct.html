Question asked Google AI - 11/2025

--- Tariffs were lowered on products recently are these items commodities with no room in the supply chain to absorb the tariff.

Following recent tariff rollbacks, particularly on food items and commodities, some of these products are considered to have little room for supply chain price absorption. Commodities like beef, coffee, bananas, oats, and certain fertilizers have been affected, with industry experts noting that unlike prior periods of lower inflation, there is now little capacity to absorb increased costs. This means that tariff changes on these items are more likely to be passed directly to consumers. 

Commodities with limited supply chain absorption

  • Beef: Record-high prices for beef have been a point of concern, and tariffs have contributed to these increased costs.

  • Coffee: The U.S. is the largest customer of coffee from Brazil, the world's largest producer. A 50% tariff on Brazilian imports, previously announced, caused coffee futures prices to rise.

  • Oats: The U.S. imports over 90% of its oats for food from Canada, the world's largest exporter. Limited domestic production means tariffs on Canadian oats have a significant impact on consumer prices for food staples.

  • Other agricultural products: Tariffs have also impacted other commodities like bananas, oranges, tomatoes, and certain fertilizers. 

  • Why supply chains have limited room for absorption

  • High inflation: Unlike in the past when inflation was lower, the current economic climate offers little room for supply chains to absorb additional costs.

  • Complex supply chains: For some commodities, particularly food items, complex and just-in-time logistics mean that changes in tariffs can lead to price increases and even shortages.

  • Lack of domestic alternatives: Some tariffed products, like oats and certain tropical fruits, have limited domestic production, meaning there is no viable domestic supply chain to replace imports.

  • Retailer pricing strategies: While some retailers may temporarily absorb costs, they are generally forced to raise prices to remain sustainable, especially for commodities with low-profit margins.
